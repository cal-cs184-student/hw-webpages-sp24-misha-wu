Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Path tracing

We first implement ray generation and primitive intersections to enable basic shading.

Because our image is pixel-based, we intuitively wish to figure out ‚Äî ‚Äúsample‚Äù ‚Äî the coloring of each pixel. At a high level, we wish to calculate the average of the colors of rays that originate at camera_pos and extend through the (x, y) pixel. Because a pixel is a unit area, the specific ‚Äúhit point‚Äù is also randomized within the contained coordinate; this allows us to obtain better results at higher pixel sampling rates.

We generate this ray by sampling a point within the (x', y') pixel in image (‚Äùraster‚Äù) space, before normalizing to the range [0, 1]. We then convert to camera space, or coordinates with the origin (0, 0) being representative of the camera‚Äôs position, by transforming with the sensor settings hFov and vFov .

Here, we then set the ray settings min_t and max_t for future parts, before normalizing and transforming into world space, or the 3D space of the objects in the scene.

We can now render colors properly; however, we do not properly render primitive objects yet. Two specific primitives we implement are triangles and spheres ; after this, we can properly render scenes composed of the above objects.

Intersections

We implement ray-triangle, at a high level, by ensuring ray-plane intersection, then ensuring the barycentric coordinates of the intersection point are within the triangle. Ray-plane intersection is valid when there is a solution to p = r(t), where p is the plane equation and r(t) = o + td is the ray equation. Similarly, a point falls within a triangle if it‚Äôs barycentric coordinates alpha, beta, gamma sum to 1 and are each within [0, 1].

In implementation, we actually use the M√∂ller‚ÄìTrumbore intersection algorithm to optimize, which simplifies the above steps into a matrix multiplication problem.

Similarly, we implement ray-sphere intersection by solving for the intersection between the ray equation and the sphere equation ‚Äî (p-c)^2 - R^2 = 0 .

Finally, we update the intersection with valid normals ‚Äî calculated for sphere intersection, interpolated with barycentric coordinates for triangle intersection ‚Äî and auxiliary shading information to enable our renderer to draw such scenes.

Part 2: BVH

Although we can now render intersected objects correctly, even the simplest scene takes multiple seconds ‚Äî far too long if we ever wish to render complex objects or incorporate physical lighting. To speed things up, we look into optimizing the data structures behind our object organization.

Specifically, we create a Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH). Currently, our rays test every object in the scene for intersection

This is a tree-like collection that groups scene objects into sets; each set is encapsulated by it‚Äôs own large ‚Äúbounding box‚Äù. Thus, a ray can eliminate groups of objects at a time by checking for intersection of each bounding box, rather than each individual primitive.

To construct the tree, we recursively evaluate the available primitives. If there are less primitives than max_leaf_size, we directly group them in a leaf node on our BVH tree; when accessed, we just iterate through the primitives. Else, we split the list by the longest axis ‚Äî this is calculated via the extent. The axis whose extent[axis] is largest gets split in half via splitting the sorted (by centroid) list. The first half of the list becomes the left node, while the right half becomes the right node. This algorithm is recursively run until all primitives end up in a leaf node.

Note that BVH algorithms with great overlap will not see as much improvement, because a ray will take much more calls to fail a bounding box test. Splitting on the longest axis ‚Äî our heuristic ‚Äî takes around 50 seconds to render ../dae/sky/dragon.dae , while splitting on the x axis (chosen arbitrarily) takes around 110 seconds to render. Thus, the splitting algorithm can also greatly affect runtimes as meshes get more complicated.

This allows renders to speed up much more; recall that our naive object organization makes every ray iterate through every object! Thus, BVH allows us to cut to O(log N) runtimes from O(n) by allowing us to quickly eliminate non-intersecting nodes. Part 3: Direct Illumination

In this section, we implement direct illumination. Direct illumination is the illumination of objects that are directly ‚Äútouched‚Äù by the lights ‚Äî think how ones hair lights up with overhead lighting.

At a high level, we follow the ray launched from the camera; on intersecting an object in the scene, we generate rays in ‚Äúrandom‚Äù new directions, performing Monte Carlo sampling. If a secondary ray hits a light, we know the origin of that ray ‚Äî the intersection point ‚Äî is illuminated by the light; the final luminance can be calculated by averaging out the samples.

However, when l=16, the portion of rays that hits the light source becomes closer to the true value, resulting in properly blurred shadows.

Part 4: Global Illumination

In this section, we focus on introducing indirect lighting into our equations, implementing global illumination.

We implement indirect lighting similar, by calculating the direct lighting at the current bounce and the recursive indirect lighting from the next bounce. Specifically, at each step we calculate the luminance of a direct bounce ‚Äî aka utilizing our previous direct lighting function as if our current ray was the first. We also calculate indirect lighting by calculating the direct lighting for the next bounce and weigh it with the reflection equation. Finally, we choose to either accumulate all results or keep only the last bounce, according to user selection.

Now, the comparison between indirect vs global lighting becomes evident!

We can see the global illumination accumulates both indirect and direct lighting.

Direct lighting dominates the bright areas of the image ‚Äî understandably, as it goes through 0 extra bounces. However, indirect lighting provides weaker illumination to affected areas, such as the blurring of the shadows and the fur. Further note that indirect lighting transfers the hit material‚Äôs properties, according to their brdfs; thus, we see color blending from the walls in parts of the rabbit and shadow.

Notice how our indirect lighting is the same as our direct lighting, albeit without the overhead light, in the first two cases.

However, in two bounces and onwards, our indirect lighting results look different. On the second bounce, we see illumination in the bunny‚Äôs shadow and lower fur, thanks to light bouncing off the walls and reflecting onto the bunny. Also note the head of the bunny looks darker; this is reasonable because there are less ‚Äúlit‚Äù areas that can hit the top of the bunny. Similarly, the third bounce reflects these findings similarly; the brightest spots from the previous bounce now reflect to the floors and corners, illuminating them.

Finally, note that the scene gets gradually darker; no matter the material, some energy is absorbed, leading to less reflected light as bounces increase.

Russian Roulette-ing

However, note that our simulation are always limited by lower #bounces; furthermore, it is wasteful to use a large # of bounces for every pixel. We can prevent this by introducing randomness into our system through Russian Roulette sampling ‚Äî at each point with bounces_left > 1, we randomly decide if we wish to terminate. This allows us to render images with a high # of bounces that remains unbiased.

We use the following command to render the following, only varying the # of bounces (-m)

./pathtracer -t 8 -s 1024 -l 16 -r 480 360 ../dae/sky/CBbunny.dae

Note how the result seem to converge by m = 3, even though we allow bounces up to 100!

Finally, we show the effects of samples per pixel on render.

The below are all run with the following command, only varying samples per pixel (-s)

./pathtracer -t 8 -l 4 -m 5 -r 480 360 ../dae/sky/CBspheres_lambertian.dae

It is visible that higher sample rates decrease noise; this makes sense as the pixel color is now an average over multiple rays, and not the result of a singular ray. It is also noticeable that, at lower sampling rates, there are more extreme colors such as pure blacks or whites. However, the average brightness of the scene remains consistent. This is because those outlier values are not averaged out, as they would be with higher sampling rates, leading to jagginess.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Finally, we implement adaptive sampling. We reiterate on the idea that it is wasteful to sample too much on areas without much change by dynamically adjusting our sampling rate.
In our implementation, we decide to stop when our samples so far converge with 96% confidence ‚Äî we calculate this by keeping a running sum of values, and check for convergence every
samplesPerBatch pixels. If convergence is satisfied, we end early; else, we keep going. It‚Äôs important to note that we now scale by the total # of pixels actually sampled, not the max # we can sample.
